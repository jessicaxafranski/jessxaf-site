MY ROLE

At Bridge, I owned the roadmap for a module integrating electronic health records with Brazil's National Health Data Network (RNDS). My core challenge was aligning regional health secretariats — each operating different legacy systems with competing priorities — around a shared data schema. I led discovery sessions with clinicians across multiple states, diagnosed that the majority of manual entry errors traced to a single workflow bottleneck, and drove the fix through a cross-functional team of engineers and compliance officers. I made the call to prioritize HL7/FHIR compliance over a faster custom integration, accepting a longer timeline to avoid technical debt that would have blocked future interoperability.

THE PROBLEM

During the COVID-19 pandemic, fragmented health data systems made coordinated response nearly impossible. Test results, vaccination records, and patient histories lived in disconnected silos across municipal and state systems. The question wasn't whether to integrate — it was how to do it without breaking trust with institutions that had failed at this before.

  • Data Fragmentation: Regional systems couldn't communicate, causing critical delays in COVID-19 test result sharing between labs and health units.
  • Clinician Friction: Field research revealed that a poorly designed data entry form was responsible for the majority of manual errors — a fixable root cause buried under assumptions about "user error."
  • Institutional Distrust: Previous integration attempts had failed; secretariats were skeptical of another top-down mandate and needed a different engagement model.

KEY DECISIONS

  • Chose HL7/FHIR over a faster custom integration Engineering wanted to build a proprietary connector to ship sooner. I pushed back — a custom layer would have broken compatibility with future RNDS updates. The slower path saved an estimated rework cycle later.
  • Fixed one form instead of redesigning the entire UI After shadowing clinicians, I identified that one specific form caused most manual errors. Instead of scoping a full overhaul (which would have taken quarters), I shipped a targeted fix in 3 weeks and measured impact before investing further.
  • Ran bilateral workshops instead of joint stakeholder meetings Two secretariats were at an impasse over data ownership. Joint sessions weren't working. I met with each separately, found a shared framing, and brought them back together with a concrete proposal — unblocking a 6-week stall.

WHAT I'D DO DIFFERENTLY

  • Define success metrics before discovery, not after I established most KPIs once the solution was already taking shape. That made it harder to argue for scope cuts later. Starting with measurable outcomes would have given me a cleaner prioritization framework from day one.
  • Involve clinicians earlier in schema decisions We looped in doctors primarily for UI feedback. But several FHIR field mapping choices created workflow friction that earlier clinical input would have caught. Data design is a UX problem too.
  • Document stakeholder agreements in writing, in real time Verbal alignment in workshops didn't always hold. Two decisions I thought were settled resurfaced as disputes weeks later. A lightweight decision log shared after each session would have prevented that.
